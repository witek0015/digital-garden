# Notes
- This research paper reviews the use of quantum dots (QDs), **semiconductor nanoparticles with unique optical properties**, in biomedical applications. 
- The authors detail QD synthesis methods, focusing on the **bottom-up approach for creating colloidal QDs** with controlled size and composition for tunable optical properties. 
- A key challenge addressed is improving QD **biocompatibility and reducing toxicity**, primarily through surface modifications like encapsulation and ligand exchange to enhance water solubility and bioconjugation for targeted delivery. 
- The paper then explores **various biomedical applications**, including ex vivo diagnostics, real-time in vivo imaging (particularly using near-infrared emitting QDs for deep tissue penetration), and therapeutic delivery of drugs and siRNA, highlighting recent advancements and future challenges in translating these technologies to clinical use.
# Highlights
- QDs are semiconducting nanoparticles with size- and composition-dependent optical and electrical properties that have attracted interest in the biomedical field for applications like bioimaging, diagnostics, drug delivery, and single molecule probes.
- The optical properties of QDs can be tuned by adjusting their size and composition. They offer advantages such as high brightness, resistance to photobleaching, multiplexing capacity, and a high surface-to-volume ratio.
- QDs have a number of advantages over traditional organic dyes and fluorescent compounds for biological applications. These include a broad absorption spectra, narrow and symmetrical emission spectra, large molar absorptivity, high quantum yield, long fluorescence excited state lifetime, large two-photon cross section, high detection sensitivity, high resistance to photobleaching, and high spectral multiplexing capability.
- Challenges in using QDs for biomedical applications include low aqueous solubility, complicated surface chemistry, low biological specificity, poorly controlled biodistribution to target tissues, and potential for severe long-term toxicity.V
- arious surface modification strategies are employed to improve solubilization, stability, and specificity. Encapsulation techniques involve coating QDs with hydrophilic materials like polymers or hydrogels.
- Ligand exchange replaces hydrophobic surface ligands with water-soluble counterparts, while bioconjugation involves attaching biomolecules (e.g., antibodies, peptides) to the QD surface.
- Biodistribution and toxicity are key concerns. QDs, being nanocolloids, are often sequestered by the reticuloendothelial system (RES) and accumulate in organs like the liver and spleen. Strategies to modify biodistribution include controlling particle size, shape, and surface properties.
- Toxicity concerns arise from the heavy metal core materials, potential release of toxic components from the surface, generation of free radicals, and nano-colloid interactions with biological systems. Cadmium-free QDs are being explored to address toxicity issues.
