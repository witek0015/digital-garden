# Notes
 - This review article discusses the current state of research in dark matter and suggests future directions. The authors, Gianfranco Bertone and Tim M. P. Tait, argue that the most popular dark matter particle candidates, such as *weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs)*, *axions*, and *sterile neutrinos*, have not been detected despite significant efforts. 
 - They suggest that the emphasis on **naturalness**, a principle that has guided WIMP research for decades, should be abandoned, and a broader approach to dark matter research should be adopted. The authors propose that astronomical observations and gravitational-wave observations can provide complementary information about the nature of dark matter and should be incorporated into research efforts. They also highlight the potential of machine learning to aid in dark matter searches. 
 - The article discusses various **alternative dark matter candidates**, including *axions*, *sterile neutrinos*, *fuzzy dark matter*, *WIMPzillas*, *primordial black holes*, and *macroscopic objects*. 
 - The authors also review research on the self-interacting nature of dark matter and the potential of astronomical observations to detect dark matter substructures. They conclude by emphasizing the importance of diversifying experimental efforts and embracing big data tools to organize the large amount of data that will be available in the future.

# Highlights
- There is a growing sense of ‘crisis’ in the dark-matter particle community, which arises from the absence of evidence for the most popular candidates for dark-matter particles—such as weakly interacting massive particles, axions and sterile neutrinos
- In the 1970s, astronomers and cosmologists began to build what is today a compelling body of evidence for this elusive component of the Universe, based on a variety of observations, including temperature anisotropies of the cosmic microwave background, baryonic acoustic oscillations, type Ia supernovae, gravitational lensing of galaxy clusters and rotation curves of galaxies
- The class of dark-matter candidates that has attracted the most attention over the past four decades is weakly interacting massive particles (WIMPs).
- The hierarchy problem is a consequence of the fact that quantum mechanics inevitably mixes up phenomena from all energy scales by allowing virtual particles to participate even in reactions whose energies are far too small to actually produce them.
- The prototypical natural theory is the minimal supersymmetric (SUSY) standard model, which introduces an additional partner for each standard-model particle. In addition, the partners of electroweak bosons are predicted to be WIMPs and thus are natural dark-matter candidates.
- Another very popular class of dark-matter candidate is that of axions and axion-like candidates. Quantum chromodynamics (QCD) axions are light, very weakly coupled particles that arise as a byproduct in theories that solve the ‘strong-CP problem’ in QCD
- However, measurements indicate that the neutron electric dipole moment is about 10−10 times smaller than expected, which necessitates a dynamical explanation. The dynamics that would cancel the neutron electric dipole moment also produces a new particle: the axion
- Another well motivated candidate is the sterile neutrino, which experiences a diluted form of the weak nuclear force through mixing with ‘ordinary’ active neutrinos. Such particles are typically included in theories that explain experiments that have found neutrinos to be massive, in contrast to the predictions of the standard model.
- Although the lifetime of a sterile neutrino playing the role of dark matter must be long enough so that the vast majority of such particles have not yet decayed, quantum mechanics dictates that some will decay more rapidly, leading to a source of mono-energetic photons with energy close to half of the neutrino mass.
- Casting a wider theoretical net offers the possibility of discovering new classes of dark-matter candidates and new experimental opportunities to search for them, and also helps assemble a ‘composite image’ of everything that we currently know about the space of possibilities that are consistent with existing measurements.
- A key property of dark matter that astronomical observations might help disproving is its collisionless nature. Dark matter self-interactions might actually help alleviate claimed tensions between numerical simulations and observations at small cosmological scales
- A generic key property of dark matter in the standard cosmological model is that it is cold—that is, non-relativistic—at the epoch of structure formation and has a free-streaming length much smaller than the size of galaxies. This implies the existence of a large number of sub-dwarf galaxy dark structures in galactic haloes.
- In addition to lens substructures, low-mass dark-matter haloes along the line of sight of the lens can act as perturbers and dominate the signal by an amount that depends on the lensing configuration and the dark-matter properties
- Interestingly, dark matter might manifest itself as a perturbation in the waveform of binary black holes. If dark matter is made of cold and collisionless particles, then their density around black holes will inevitably be higher (possibly much higher) than their average density in the Universe.
