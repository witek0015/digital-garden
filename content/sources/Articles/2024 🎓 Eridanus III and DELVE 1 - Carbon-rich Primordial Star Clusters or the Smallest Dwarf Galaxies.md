# Notes
- **Two mysterious Milky Way satellites**, Eridanus III and DELVE 1, were studied using spectroscopy. Each has 8 confirmed member stars.
- The **brightest stars in both systems are extremely metal-poor**: Fe/H = –3.1 for Eri III and –2.8 for DELVE 1.
- These stars show **high carbon enrichment and low barium/strontium content**, classifying them as **CEMP-no stars**, not typically found in globular clusters.
- Eri III and DELVE 1 are **very compact** (half-light radii: 8 pc and 6 pc), smaller than any known dwarf galaxy, but chemically distinct from known globular clusters.
- **Chemical abundance patterns resemble those of ultra-faint dwarf galaxies**, raising the possibility that these are the **smallest known galaxies**.
- **Dynamical mass estimates are uncertain**, and current data **cannot confirm the presence of dark matter**, leaving their classification ambiguous.
- If not galaxies, they might represent a **new class of primordial star clusters** formed early in cosmic history, possibly from material enriched by a single Population III supernova.
# Highlights
**Definitions:**
	- **CEMP-no stars** = Carbon-Enhanced Metal-Poor stars with no significant neutron-capture elements.
	- **Ultra-faint dwarfs** = Very low-luminosity galaxies, often rich in dark matter.
**Objects:**
	- **Eri III:** MV ≈ –2.1, r_half ≈ 8.6 pc
	- **DELVE 1:** MV ≈ 0.2, r_half ≈ 6.2 pc
**Key Properties:**
	- Both are chemically similar to **dwarf galaxies** (metal-poor, C-enhanced)
	- But physically **as compact as star clusters**
	- **Ambiguous classification**: Possibly faint galaxies, or **"fossil" clusters** from early star formation.
**Future Needs:**
	- Better **velocity dispersion measurements** and **larger samples** of chemical abundances to settle their true nature.
