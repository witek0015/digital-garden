# Notes
- This academic article presents a framework for analyzing how video games utilize historical elements and the impact this has on players' understanding of history. 
- The authors, Metzger and Paxton, propose a typology of "deployments" – ways games represent the past, including Antiquarian (focus on realism), Monumental (glorification), Critical (alternative narratives), Wishstory (idealized versions), Composite Imagination (blending historical elements), Borrowed Authenticity (using history for a fantasy setting), Historical Provenance (connecting past to present issues), and Legitimization (emphasizing historical accuracy). 
- These deployments are shaped by overarching paradigms of "gamification" (adapting history for gameplay) and "historical boundary structures" (what aspects of history are deemed playable). 
- The article's purpose is to provide researchers and educators with a vocabulary to understand the intersection of formal history education and the informal learning experiences offered by video games, arguing that history educators should be aware of how games shape students' understanding of the past.

# Highlights
- The authors present a framework of seven deployments of history that describe how video games utilize historical elements. These deployments can be found in games that aim to recreate the past as well as games that utilize history for purely entertainment purposes.●
- The authors also identify two overarching paradigms that limit how video games use history.
	- Gamification of the past highlights the need for developers to prioritize playability over historical accuracy. This leads to the use of tropes like the quest structure, deterministic technological progress, and militarization, which may result in an oversimplified or distorted view of the past.
	- Historical boundary structures describe the limits on which aspects of history can be incorporated into a game. Game developers are influenced by contemporary social and cultural norms and the need to avoid controversial content. 
- These paradigms and deployments are not mutually exclusive; a single game element might reflect several different deployments or be influenced by both the need for gamification and the limitations imposed by historical boundary structures.
- The authors acknowledge that, like readers of print texts, players respond to video games in different ways. A player's prior knowledge and individual identity can shape how they understand and interpret a game's historical content.
- The authors suggest that their framework can be a useful tool for educators who want to understand what their students are learning from video games and how these games might be shaping their understanding of history
