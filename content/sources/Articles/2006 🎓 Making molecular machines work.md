# Notes
- This 2006 _Nature Nanotechnology_ review article by Browne and Feringa surveys the burgeoning field of synthetic molecular machines. 
- The authors detail recent advancements in controlling molecular motion, focusing on **design principles for linear and rotary motors**, and comparing various approaches to constructing wholly synthetic molecular machines, such as **rotors, elevators, and valves**. 
- A central theme is overcoming the challenges of **Brownian motion** to achieve **directional control** and ultimately, **macroscopic work** from concerted molecular motion. 
- The article concludes by discussing the potential applications and future directions of this rapidly developing nanotechnology.
# Highlights
- The ultimate goal of nanotechnology research is to design and create synthetic molecular machines that can perform useful functions. The authors take inspiration from biological motors, which are essential for many biological processes, but note that wholly synthetic systems are more stable and can operate in more diverse conditions.
- A central challenge in developing molecular machines is controlling their motion, especially directionality, in the face of Brownian motion. Brownian motion, the random thermal motion of molecules, presents a significant obstacle to the controlled operation of molecular machines5 The authors discuss various strategies for addressing this challenge, including exploiting Brownian motion using Brownian ratchets, immobilizing machines on surfaces, and constructing larger machines that can overcome Brownian motion.
- Researchers have made significant progress in developing molecular machines capable of both rotary and linear motion. The article highlights examples of molecular rotors, some of which can achieve rotation speeds of up to 100 MHz in the solid state. The authors also discuss examples of rotaxane-based systems that exhibit linear motion, with some systems achieving movement up to 1.5 nm with a frequency of 10 kHz.
- Light is a particularly promising fuel for powering molecular machines, as it allows for continuous unidirectional rotary motion. The authors describe light-driven unidirectional rotary motors that operate by a four-step process involving the cis-trans isomerization of a carbon-carbon double bond. These motors have been engineered to achieve rotation speeds of up to 80 revolutions per second at 20 °C.
- Immobilizing molecular machines on surfaces is crucial for integrating them into devices and for realizing macroscopic effects. The authors discuss examples of molecular rotors immobilized on gold surfaces, demonstrating the feasibility of surface-mounted molecular machines.13 They also highlight the challenges associated with surface immobilization, such as the influence of connecting units on the functionality of the motor.
- Researchers have demonstrated the ability to translate molecular motion into macroscopic movement, opening up possibilities for applications in various fields. The article describes examples of systems where molecular machines have been used to control surface wettability, transport liquid droplets, and bend microcantilever beams. These examples highlight the potential of molecular machines to perform useful work and to influence macroscopic properties.
- Molecular valves, which can control the flow of substances at the nanoscale, are a promising application of molecular machines. The authors discuss examples of light- and redox-controlled nanovalves based on channel proteins, cyclophanes, and rotaxanes. These nanovalves have potential applications in drug delivery, sensors, and nanofluidic systems.
