# Notes
- Short history of radio transmitting. This is an introduction to a broader series of articles focusing on different kinds of wireless transmitting.
# Highlights
- Radio has been part of our technology since Marconi first ‘‘loaded’’ an antenna to transmit an electromagnetic signal Fig. 1: Marconi receiving the first signals from his transmitter and receiver invention circa 1901 to a receiver
- Signaling and audio communication using electromagnetic radiation was first utilized as a wireless telegraph where telegraph lines were impractical or unreliable. Electromagnetic radiation is the formal term that was introduced by Heinrich Hertz, but other terms were quickly instituted to describe early radio.
- Building on the technology of the telegraph, early spark radios sent Morse code. First voice transmissions were made in 1900 transmitting weather information over a distance of one mile. The first transatlantic voice communication was sent by Marconi in 1901. Ship to shore communication followed quickly in 1902.
- The tragedy of the Titanic in April of 1912 played a large role in the exposure of radio to the American public. The Carpathia had received the SOS signals sent by the radio operator of the Titanic and steamed to the location rescuing the few survivors.
- The term radio was originally a prefix meaning radiation and was used in radio-activity to describe rays emitted by decaying atoms. The term was then attached to telegraphy in an issue of Electrical Review in 1906, ‘radio-telegraphy’. Eventually, these terms were shortened to just plain radio.
- Radio expanded during the 1930s and 1940s reaching its commercial peak in the 1950s, being challenged by television. But, television is still wireless sending much more information over a given channel.
