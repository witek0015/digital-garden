# Notes
- The study found that acute exercise elicits timing-specific effects on adipose tissue metabolism, independently of feeding status. Adipose tissue is more sensitive to exercise during the early active phase.
- Molecular clock rhythmicity in adipose tissue was not disrupted by exercise3. However, exercise during the early active phase increased serum nonesterified fatty acids (NEFA), indicating an increase in lipolysis. This effect was not observed during the early rest phase
- Early active phase exercise altered the transcriptomic profile of inguinal white adipose tissue (iWAT), upregulating genes associated with thermogenesis, mitochondrial proliferation, and hormone signaling
- In vitro experiments with synchronized 3T3-L1 adipocytes showed timing-dependent differences in adrenergic receptor expression and lipolytic activity, suggesting a cell-autonomous circadian clock influence on adipocyte metabolism
- The time-of-day-specific transcriptomic response to exercise in iWAT is not governed by feeding status11. Fasting during the early rest phase did not replicate the gene expression changes observed with early active phase exercise
- The study suggests that exercise timing may fine-tune adipose metabolism to improve energy homeostasis, and further research is needed to determine whether timing of exercise can improve energy homeostasis in obesity or type 2 diabetes
