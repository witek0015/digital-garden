# Notes
- This is an excerpt from a 2017 chemistry review article celebrating 50 years of supramolecular chemistry, a field born from Charles Pedersen's discovery of crown ethers. 
- The article highlights the field's remarkable progress, including two Nobel Prizes, and showcases its diverse practical applications. These applications span **supramolecular sensors** for various analytes, **molecular imaging** in medicine using rotaxanes, **metal extraction** from ores and nuclear waste utilizing host-guest chemistry, and **drug formulation and delivery** employing cyclodextrins and cucurbit[n]urils. 
- The authors' aim is to demonstrate the significant real-world impact of supramolecular chemistry beyond purely academic research.

# Highlights
- Supramolecular chemistry's growth and impact: The field of supramolecular chemistry has rapidly expanded over the past 50 years, garnering two Nobel Prizes and impacting various applications like sensing, imaging, metal extraction, and drug delivery12. The ability to control and manipulate interactions between molecules has opened doors to new technologies and solutions across various fields.
- Sensing applications: Supramolecular chemistry has enabled the development of sensitive and selective sensors for various analytes, such as ions and glucose34. This has led to advancements like real-time blood ion concentration monitoring and continuous glucose monitoring for diabetic patients.
- Advancements in molecular imaging: Integrating supramolecular chemistry with imaging techniques has led to the creation of novel imaging agents, particularly utilizing squaraine rotaxanes7. These advancements hold promise for improving the accuracy and effectiveness of medical imaging in preclinical and potentially clinical settings.
- Metal extraction and recovery: Supramolecular chemistry plays a crucial role in hydrometallurgy, allowing for the selective extraction of metals from ores8. Specifically, the use of phenolic oximes has significantly impacted copper recovery, contributing to approximately 25% of global copper production as of 2013. This highlights the practical applications of supramolecular chemistry in addressing resource challenges.
- Drug delivery and formulation: Cyclodextrins, a family of supramolecular hosts, are widely used in drug delivery due to their biocompatibility and ability to enhance drug solubility and bioavailability910. The use of substituted cyclodextrins like HPBCD and SBEBCD has allowed for improved drug formulations, leading to better treatment options.
- Emergence of cucurbit(n)urils: Cucurbit(n)urils (CB(n)s), particularly acyclic variants, have shown significant potential as drug delivery hosts. These molecules exhibit excellent biocompatibility and can significantly enhance the solubility of poorly soluble drugs, offering a promising alternative to cyclodextrins in the future of drug delivery
