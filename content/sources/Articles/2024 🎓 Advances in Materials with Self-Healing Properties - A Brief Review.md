# Notes
- This is a 2024 review article by Rashid Dallaev exploring advancements in self-healing materials (SHMs). 
- The article surveys recent progress across various material types—**polymers, ceramics, metals, and composites**—highlighting their unique self-repair mechanisms. 
- A key focus is on **crack healing processes** and the use of **microcapsules** as healing agents, along with the potential of a "vascular system" approach. 
- The review further details applications of SHMs in diverse fields, including **biomedicine (implants and prostheses)**, aerospace, and construction, concluding with future research directions and implications for sustainability.

# Highlights
- Self-healing materials (SHMs) are a class of smart materials that can repair damage autonomously or with minimal external intervention. SHMs draw inspiration from the self-repair capabilities observed in biological systems. These materials have the potential to increase the lifespan of products, reduce maintenance costs, and improve safety.
- SHMs have a variety of applications across different industries, including:
	- Electronics: Self-healing coatings for mobile phones can prevent breakage and scratches.
	- Construction: Self-healing concrete can repair cracks, enhancing the longevity and structural integrity of buildings.
	- Biomedicine: Self-healing polymers are being used in implants, prostheses, and drug delivery systems.
	- Textiles: Self-healing fabrics can repair punctures and tears at room temperature.
- Several mechanisms contribute to self-healing in materials, including:
	- Microcapsules: These capsules, embedded within a material, contain healing agents that are released upon damage.
	- Vascular networks: Mimicking biological circulatory systems, microchannels within the material transport healing agents to damaged areas.
	- Intrinsic chemical reactions: Some materials can self-heal through reversible chemical bonds or reactions triggered by external stimuli like heat or light.
- Polymers are at the forefront of SHM research due to their ability to restore intermolecular bonds and form new ones. Some polymers can self-heal quickly and efficiently, even at room temperature.
- Self-healing ceramics and concrete utilize different mechanisms, often relying on oxidation reactions or the growth of reinforcing crystals to fill cracks. The development of bio concrete, which uses bacteria to repair damage, is a promising area of research.
- Despite the significant progress in SHM development, challenges remain, including:
	- Scalability and Cost: Producing SHMs on a large scale can be expensive, limiting their wider adoption.
	- Environmental Impact: Some SHMs rely on materials or processes that may have environmental concerns.
	- Integration: Incorporating SHMs into existing infrastructure and manufacturing processes presents technical hurdles.●
- Future research in SHMs is focused on:
	- Tailoring materials to specific applications: This includes considering factors like environmental conditions, mechanical properties, and biocompatibility.
	- Developing multifunctional SHMs: This involves combining self-healing with other functionalities like sensing or antimicrobial properties.
	- Sustainable production: Exploring environmentally friendly materials and manufacturing processes is crucial for the long-term viability of SHMs.
	- Translational research: Bridging the gap between laboratory research and practical applications is key to realizing the full potential of SHMs.
