# Notes
- This research paper explores the burgeoning field of sustainable nanocatalysis, focusing on the shift from scarce and expensive noble metals to more abundant alternatives. 
- The authors highlight recent advancements in utilizing earth-abundant metals, either alone, in minute combinations with noble metals, or activated by light, for various organic transformations.
- Key strategies discussed include enhancing the reactivity of abundant metals through nanomaterial design, minimizing the use of precious metals via techniques like single-atom catalysis, and exploiting plasmon resonance for photoactivation.
- Ultimately, the paper advocates for the development of cost-effective, environmentally benign catalysts based on earth-abundant metals to meet growing industrial and environmental demands.
# Highlights
- The use of noble metals in catalysis presents sustainability challenges due to their scarcity, cost, and toxicity. Researchers are increasingly focusing on developing catalysts based on earth-abundant metals to address these concerns.
- Earth-abundant metals generally exhibit lower reactivity compared to noble metals, and are also prone to issues like oxidizability and deactivation during catalysis.3 To overcome these challenges, scientists are exploring strategies like synthesizing well-defined nanomaterials to enhance activity, and using photoactivation to improve catalytic performance.
- One approach to sustainable nanocatalysis involves significantly reducing the amount of precious metal required for catalytic activity.4 Researchers have achieved this by creating catalysts that contain only minute quantities of noble metals combined with earth-abundant metals.14 For example, Gong et al. developed a Pt-ZnO/Al2O3 catalyst for propane dehydrogenation that exhibits high activity and propylene selectivity while using only 20% of the platinum found in conventional systems.
- Another promising strategy is single-atom catalysis (SAC), which aims to utilize every atom of the precious metal as a potential active site.6 SAC involves stabilizing single atoms of a noble metal on a support material, and the strong interaction between the atom and the support plays a crucial role in catalytic activity.67 Abbet et al. demonstrated the potential of SAC by showing that a single palladium atom on MgO can effectively catalyze the cyclotrimerization of acetylene at low temperatures.
- The conversion of biomass-derived platform molecules like 5-hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF) into valuable chemicals and fuels is another area where earth-abundant metal catalysts are showing promise.9 While precious metals like Pt, Ru, and Pd have traditionally been used for HMF conversion, researchers have successfully employed bimetallic systems like nickel-tungsten carbide and copper-cobalt nanoparticles to selectively produce 2,5-dimethylfuran (DMF) from HMF.
- The use of plasmon-enhanced catalysis with earth-abundant metals like aluminum and copper offers an innovative approach to sustainable organic transformations. Plasmonic nanomaterials exhibit strong absorption in the visible region due to surface plasmon resonance (SPR), leading to the generation of highly active sites for redox reactions. Halas’ group utilized aluminum nanocrystals with palladium islands as a catalyst for the selective hydrogenation of acetylene to ethylene under white light illumination, demonstrating the ability of SPR to influence catalytic selectivity. Linic's group addressed the challenge of copper oxidation by employing visible light irradiation to restore the active copper surface for selective propylene epoxidation.
