# Notes
- This paper offers a multi-faceted perspective on the ongoing quest to understand high-temperature superconductivity, a phenomenon defying explanation by conventional theories. The paper presents a collection of viewpoints from leading researchers, each highlighting a specific area of focus and their contributions to the field. 
- The key themes emerge as: 
	- (1) **the intricate many-body interactions** governing the behavior of electrons in these materials, demanding sophisticated experimental techniques like ARPES and RIXS to unravel, 
	- (2) **the breakdown of traditional Fermi liquid theory** in these unconventional superconductors, necessitating the exploration of alternative models, and 
	- (3) **the role of competing orders** and their influence on the emergence of superconductivity. 
- The paper advocates for the development of an **"integrated spectroscopy"** approach, combining multiple experimental techniques and computational methods to address this complex challenge, while also underscoring the potential of **quantum geometry and topology** in guiding the search for new high-temperature superconductors. 
- Ultimately, the paper emphasizes the need for a comprehensive understanding of the **"Mottness"** of these materials, characterized by strong electron-electron interactions, to unlock the secrets of high-temperature superconductivity and potentially pave the way for room-temperature superconductivity.

# Highlights
- Superconductors with high superconducting transition temperatures (Tc ) have unusual properties that cannot be explained by conventional Fermi liquid theory or the Bardeen– Cooper–Schrieffer (BCS) theory of superconductivity
- Over the past three decades, angleresolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES) has played a key role in unveiling the electronic structure and gap structure of high-temperature superconductors.
- ARPES can directly measure the electron self-energy, which is intimately related to electron scattering and electron pairing. Recently, it has become possible to extract the pairing Eliashberg function from ARPES measurements
- One of the characteristics of cuprate superconductors is the existence of multiple orders, which may compete or even be intertwined (that is, they share the same origin but manifest differently) with each other
- X-ray scattering, which is closely related to the density–density correlation function, has firmly established the universal existence of a charge density wave (CDW) order in cuprates
- The next frontier is to investigate how to ‘engineer’ the correlation functions of spin, lattice and charges via sample manipulations that can control the competing orders under both equilibrium and non-equilibrium conditions.
- Ab initio numerical studies have verified stable superconducting phases that are quantitatively consistent with the experimental phase diagram of the cuprates and Fe-based superconductors without adjustable parameters beyond the Hubbard model (used to describe the Mott transition).
- . A consistent picture has emerged, indicating the presence of a strong effective attraction between electrons driven by Mott physics. This attraction works as a ‘double-edged sword’, as the origin of both the superconductivity and the charge inhomogeneity
- Conventionally, superconductivity in metals has been explained by the formation of Cooper pairs, caused by electron–phonon interactions at low temperatures. Cooper pairs follow Bose–Einstein statistics, which enables the pairs to condense into a stable ground state below the Fermi level and become phase coherent
- In 1986, superconductivity was observed in cuprates and their critical temperature (Tc) can reach up to 135K. Dubbed ‘strange metals’, cuprates cannot be described by the Fermi liquid model. A number of features are typical of these strange metals
- Another feature of cuprates is the pseudogap phase (a gap seen only by some electrons but not others), which appears in the electronic bandstructure even above the Tc. One possible explanation for this is that Cooper pairs have formed — opening a gap near the Fermi level — but have not yet become phase coherent and therefore do not superconduct. T
- A central concept in condensed matter physics is the Fermi surface. It is the momentum-space locus of low-energy fermionic excitations in a metallic state.
- . What is required in constructing a new theory to explain unconventional superconductivity is some kind of new fixed point — a point in theory space where we know precisely which interactions either destroy or maintain Mott physics.
- There is a symbiotic relationship between the search for a theory of high-Tc superconductivity and quantum spin liquids. One theory for high-Tc superconductivity suggests the existence of chargons — the bosonic charge component of an electron — which can condense into a superconducting phase
- One of the main research themes is to derive microscopic model Hamiltonians that capture the essential physics behind complex quantum materials.
- A dream of roomtemperature superconductivity motivated the field of superconductivity, but the BCS understanding of superconductivity required phonon-mediated pairing of electrons, which did not seem possible at high temperatures. When high-Tc superconductivity was discovered 35 years ago, BCS theory was unable to account for it, and a microscopic theory of these unconventional superconductors is not yet established
- In 2015, we serendipitously found an ‘Earth temperature’ superconductivity of 203K in H3S at a pressure of 150GPa (ref.10). Later, superconductivity at 250−260K in LaH10 was found, and, recently, superconductivity at 287K was announced in a material that is likely to be H3S modified by carbon.
