# Notes
- This academic article explores the emergence of a new political cleavage centered around globalization. 
- The authors argue that **ideology**, specifically **cosmopolitanism** and **communitarianism**, plays a crucial role in shaping this cleavage. They propose a framework analyzing how philosophical debates about justice—between **globalists** and **statists**, and **universalists** and **contextualists**—manifest in public discourse, focusing on four key areas of contention: **border permeability, authority allocation, community value, and justification patterns.** 
- The ultimate goal is to provide a conceptual tool for future empirical research examining the ideological foundations of globalization conflicts and their impact on democratic processes.

# Highlights
- The authors argue that **globalization can lead to the formation of a new political cleavage** between those who support further globalization (cosmopolitans) and those who advocate for a return to national sovereignty (communitarians).
- Ideology is an essential component of cleavage formation. Without a coherent normative framework, conflicts about globalization will likely remain fragmented and difficult to understand.
- Political entrepreneurs often draw on political philosophy to develop and disseminate the core components of their ideologies, giving them a normative underpinning and strengthening their mobilizing power.
- Cosmopolitanism draws on the arguments of globalists and universalists and supports open borders, global authorities, individualism, and individual rights as the primary frame of justice.
- Communitarianism draws on the arguments of statists and contextualists and supports meaningful borders, state or group sovereignty, community life, and collective needs and self-determination.
- The authors propose a conceptual framework for analyzing the extent to which cosmopolitanism and communitarianism are manifest in public discourse about globalization.
- The framework identifies four principled conflicts: the permeability of borders, the allocation of authority to political institutions, the normative value of community, and valid justifications for political claims.
