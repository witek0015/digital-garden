*Christopher Buehlman*
### review
_Between Two Fires_ was an absolute blast—if by “blast” you mean a gut-wrenching, plague-ridden, demon-haunted descent into the bowels of medieval despair... and I do. From the very first page, the book hits that rare and magical tone where **biblical apocalypse meets emotional sincerity**, and somehow makes that combo feel effortless. The setting? Diseased and damned. The tone? Pure dread. The experience? Sublime.

What really sold it for me was the **consistency of the character writing**. Thomas, the disgraced knight, is complex without ever tipping into melodrama, and the girl—holy and eerie in equal measure—never becomes a trope. Every character arc unfolds like it was grown, not written—nothing feels forced or theatrical, just painfully real, even when there are literal angels and monsters crashing through the narrative. That’s not easy to pull off in a book with such **supernatural weight**, but Buehlman does it like it’s no big deal.

I found this gem thanks to a _Dark Souls_ recommendation, and honestly? Nailed it. It carries that same sense of crumbling beauty, of faith shattered and reforged in blood, of hopelessness stalked by something worse. The world is cruel, quiet, and occasionally pierced by the kind of horrifying grace that makes you put the book down for a second just to exhale.

The language deserves a nod, too. It’s **easy to read**, accessible in a way that makes the story fly—but still evocative, poetic even, when it needs to hit hard. Is it historically accurate medieval-speak? Absolutely not. Do I care? Not even slightly. I’d take Buehlman’s smooth, slightly modern style over faux-ye-olde gibberish any day.

In short: _Between Two Fires_ is grim, gorgeous, and unrelenting—a **divine horror journey through hell on earth**, and one of the rare books that actually earns its comparisons to the likes of _Dark Souls_ or _The Road_. I went in for plague and demons; I stayed for broken men, quiet miracles, and the burning question of what’s still worth saving in a ruined world.

### context
- **General info**: Between Two Fires is a dark historical fantasy/horror novel published in 2012 by Christopher Buehlman, a poet and performer turned novelist known for his atmospheric and unsettling stories.

- **Setting**: The book is set during 14th-century France, specifically during the time of the Black Death, blending historical realism with apocalyptic horror rooted in Christian mythology. The novel takes place during the mid-1300s, a time ravaged by plague, war, famine, and religious upheaval—the perfect storm for supernatural dread.

- **Tone and Genre**: Buehlman taps into the brutal reality of medieval Europe and mixes it with a cosmic battle between Heaven and Hell, suggesting that the physical horrors of the world are just a symptom of something far darker and more ancient. This is grimdark at its finest—bleak, violent, morally grey, but layered with poetic moments and flashes of strange beauty.


- **Author**: Known for his theatrical background and lyrical prose, Buehlman writes horror with literary ambition. His other works often feature flawed protagonists, dark humor, and mythological or folkloric horror—but Between Two Fires is widely regarded as one of his most powerful and haunting novels.

- **Themes**: Central themes include faith in a godless world, redemption, and the corruptibility of humanity, all examined through a journey filled with violence, loss, and supernatural encounters.

The story focuses on a fallen knight, a mysterious girl, and their journey through a landscape filled with plague and demonic influence—a pilgrimage through Hell masquerading as Earth.

Between Two Fires has become a cult favorite among fans of historical horror and dark fantasy, praised for its rich writing, vivid world-building, and emotional weight.

It’s particularly loved for its unique blending of medieval history and supernatural horror, a combo that sets it apart from more traditional genre fiction.
